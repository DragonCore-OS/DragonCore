# Why Dragon? — A Cross-Lingual Naming Note

## The Chinese Word for Lobster

In English, "lobster" is just lobster.  
But in Chinese, the word for lobster is **龙虾**:

- **龙** (lóng) = dragon  
- **虾** (xiā) = shrimp

This creates a cultural contrast that does not exist in English.

In Chinese, 龙虾 does not evoke the majesty of a dragon.  
It is, quite literally, a **shrimp with a dragon-like name**.

## What DragonCore Means

When we say **DragonCore**, we are making a specifically Chinese distinction:

Not something that merely **borrows** the name of the dragon,  
but something built around what the dragon **represents** in Chinese civilization:

| Dragon Symbolizes | What We Built |
|-------------------|---------------|
| Order | Layered governance (三省六部) |
| Legitimacy | Traceable authority and final arbitration |
| Coordination | Multi-agent deliberation, not chaotic competition |
| Continuity | Archive, inheritance, civilizational memory |
| Adaptability | Escalation, rollback, executable recovery |

## The Core Difference

| | 龙虾 (Lobster) | 龙核 (DragonCore) |
|---|----------------|-------------------|
| **Form** | Hard shell, many claws | Integrated system |
| **Movement** | Sideways, scattered | Coordinated, purposeful |

## In Chinese, We Say

**真龙，不是龙虾。**

*True Dragon. Not a shrimp with a dragon name.*

Or, in our brand shorthand:

**Dragon, not Claw.**

## Why This Matters for AI

Most multi-agent systems today are built like **lobsters**:
- Flat, tool-using, grasping at tasks
- No clear authority chain
- No traceable decisions
- No memory, no continuity

DragonCore is built like a **dragon**:
- Governed, layered, traceable
- Authority separated and auditable
- Decisions escalate and resolve
- Production runs are ledgered and archived

That is the difference between **claw-first** and **governance-first**.
